If you've tried growing microgreens at home, you've probably met the enemy: a fuzzy white web creeping across your tray, usually a day or two before harvest. It's discouraging, a little gross, and the single most common reason people give up on growing their own greens.

The good news: mold is preventable once you understand what causes it. The frustrating news: preventing it by hand means controlling several variables at once, every single day. Let's break down both — and then talk honestly about why it keeps happening anyway.

## First, is it mold — or root hairs?

Before you toss a tray, make sure you're not looking at **root hairs**. New growers panic over a fine, fuzzy halo around the base of the stems and assume the worst. Here's how to tell them apart:

| | Root hairs (harmless) | Mold (a problem) |
|---|---|---|
| **Where** | Only around roots / stem base | Spreads across surface and up stems |
| **Look** | Fine, even, fringe-like | Web-like or cottony, patchy |
| **Color** | White | White, gray, green, or black |
| **Smell** | None | Musty, sour |
| **Water test** | Lies flat when misted | Unaffected |

When in doubt: a quick spritz of water makes root hairs lie down; mold doesn't care.

## What actually causes microgreens to mold

Mold spores are everywhere — in your seeds, your air, your kitchen. They only become a problem when the environment lets them outcompete your seedlings. Four conditions do that:

1. **Too much moisture, too little airflow.** Stagnant, humid air is mold's ideal home. Trays in a closed cabinet or covered too long are the classic culprit.
2. **Overcrowded seeds.** Pile seeds on top of each other and the dense mat traps moisture against the medium — no air gets in.
3. **Warm, stuffy temperatures.** Most molds thrive in the same 70–80°F range microgreens like, so warmth without ventilation accelerates both.
4. **Contaminated seeds or dirty equipment.** Reusing trays without sanitizing, or using seeds not intended for sprouting, stacks the deck against you.

## How to prevent mold (the manual checklist)

Here's the standard advice — and it works, *if* you do all of it, consistently:

| Step | What to do | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Seed density | Don't overseed; use the recommended amount | Crowding traps moisture |
| Airflow | Run a small fan on low nearby | Moving air starves mold |
| Watering | Water from the **bottom** tray, not the leaves | A dry canopy resists mold |
| Blackout timing | Uncover on time (usually day 3–4) | Covered-too-long = mold |
| Sanitation | Wash trays with diluted hydrogen peroxide or vinegar between grows | Kills carried-over spores |
| Climate | Keep things cool-ish and well-ventilated | Heat + stuffiness speeds mold |

Do all six, every grow, and your mold rate drops sharply.

## Why it still happens — and why that's the real problem

Here's the honest truth most growing guides skip: **mold prevention is a daily balancing act of moisture, airflow, temperature, and timing — and a single off day can undo a whole tray.**

You go out of town. You forget to refill the water. The kitchen gets warm during a heat wave. The blackout cover stays on one day too long. Any one of those, and you're back to the fuzzy web — often right before harvest, after you've already put in nine days.

That's not a knowledge problem. It's a *consistency* problem. And consistency over 10+ days is exactly what humans are bad at and machines are good at. It's why so many people's microgreens story is "I tried it once, it molded, I gave up."
