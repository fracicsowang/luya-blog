If you've fallen for microgreens, you've probably hit the same fork in the road everyone does: should you grow them yourself, or just buy them at the store? The grow vs buy microgreens question really comes down to three things people care about most — what it costs, how fresh the greens are when they reach your plate, and how much work you're honestly signing up for.

This guide breaks down all three with real numbers, including the downsides of each path that most articles skip. By the end, you'll know which option fits your kitchen, your schedule, and your budget — and where the trade-offs actually bite.

## What You're Actually Comparing

Before the math, it helps to remember why microgreens earn a place on your counter at all. According to peer-reviewed reviews (MDPI Plants) and USDA data, these tiny seedlings are roughly 2 to 3.5 times more nutrient-dense than their full-grown counterparts, with radish microgreens often topping the charts. Some go further: Johns Hopkins research found broccoli microgreens carry 10 to 100 times more of the sulforaphane precursor than mature broccoli heads — one analysis measured around 633 mg of total sulforaphane per 100 g of fresh broccoli microgreens.

So whether you grow or buy, you're getting a serious nutritional upgrade over the average salad. They're also rich in vitamins C, K, and E plus a range of antioxidant polyphenols. (If you want the full picture, our guide on [microgreens nutrition](/microgreens-nutrition/) goes deep, and [are microgreens good for you](/are-microgreens-good-for-you/) tackles the common skeptic questions.)

The real decision isn't whether microgreens are worth eating — it's logistics. Buying is instant but carries two well-known pain points. Growing is rewarding but asks something of you every single day. Let's look at each honestly.

## The Cost: Grow vs Buy Microgreens by the Numbers

Store-bought microgreens are not cheap. A typical 2 oz clamshell runs $4 to $6, which works out to roughly $30 to $50 per pound. For a food you'd happily eat by the handful, that adds up fast — a daily handful from the store can quietly become a $40-plus monthly habit. (We dig into why in [store-bought microgreens: expensive and not fresh](/store-bought-microgreens-expensive-not-fresh/).)

Growing your own can dramatically lower the per-serving cost. Seeds are cheap by the ounce, a single tray yields several servings, and the marginal cost of each new batch is mostly just seed and water. The catch is the upfront effort, the recurring attention, and the failure rate (more on that below). Here's how the two paths stack up.

| Factor | Buying (clamshell) | Growing by hand |
|---|---|---|
| Cost per 2 oz | $4–$6 | A fraction of retail |
| Cost per pound | ~$30–$50 | Far below $30 |
| Time to plate | Instant | ~7–10 day cycle |
| Freshness at eating | Days old | Minutes old |
| Daily effort | None | Watering + checks, ~10 days |
| Failure risk | None | Real (mold, missed watering) |
| Shelf life | Short, wilts fast | Harvest only what you need |
| Mess | None | Soil on counter and sink |

![Chart: cost per pound of store-bought versus home-grown microgreens](/img/charts/cost-store-vs-grow.svg)

The headline is simple: buying is convenient but expensive, while growing trades money for time and attention. Whether that trade pays off depends entirely on one thing most cost comparisons ignore — how reliably you can actually keep a tray alive over its full cycle.

## The Freshness Gap

This is where buying really struggles. Store-bought microgreens travel a long road: farm to truck to warehouse to store shelf to your fridge. By the time you buy them, they're often several days old — and microgreens wilt fast. The result is short shelf life, mushy leaves, dull color, and a surprising amount of waste when half the clamshell turns before you finish it. (We unpack this fully in [are store-bought microgreens fresh](/are-store-bought-microgreens-fresh/).)

Freshness isn't just about taste and crunch. With nutrient-dense greens, fresher genuinely matters at the chemical level. Take sulforaphane, the compound that makes broccoli microgreens so interesting: it's an isothiocyanate that only forms when the precursor glucoraphanin meets the enzyme myrosinase as you chew or chop the greens raw. That reaction activates the body's Nrf2 antioxidant and detox pathway, which is why sulforaphane is being studied (still at the research stage, not as a proven cure) for blood-sugar regulation, inflammation, and cellular defense.

The practical takeaway: heat and overcooking degrade myrosinase, and time on a shelf doesn't help either. Raw, fresh, and well-chewed is how you get the most out of them. (For the deeper science, see [what is sulforaphane](/what-is-sulforaphane/) and [maximize sulforaphane](/maximize-sulforaphane/).) So the closer to harvest you eat, the better — which is exactly where home growing wins.

Growing at home flips the freshness equation completely. You snip what you need seconds before it hits your plate. Nothing sits in plastic losing crunch, color, and nutrients. That "alive until you eat it" quality is the single strongest argument for growing your own.

## The Catch with DIY Growing

Growing microgreens by hand sounds simple, and the easiest crops really are beginner-friendly — radish germinates in 1 to 2 days and is ready to harvest in 5 to 7, with pea shoots, broccoli, and sunflower close behind. (Curious where to start? See [easiest microgreens to grow](/easiest-microgreens-to-grow/).)

But anyone who's run manual trays for a few months knows the three M's that quietly raise the true cost of "free" greens:

- **Mold.** The number-one killer of home trays. Too much moisture, poor airflow, overcrowded seeds, and a warm room create the perfect storm. One fuzzy tray and the whole batch goes in the trash — there's no rinsing it off. (Here's [why microgreens keep molding](/why-microgreens-keep-molding/) and how to spot it early.)
- **Mess.** Soil on the counter, in the sink, tracked across the kitchen. For tidy kitchens, this alone is a real deterrent — and it's why some growers experiment with [growing microgreens without soil](/grow-microgreens-without-soil/).
- **Maintenance.** This is the quiet one, and it sinks more trays than any other. Microgreens need daily watering and attention across roughly 10-day cycles. Travel for a weekend, get slammed at work, or simply forget for two days, and the tray is gone. Manual growing has a genuine failure rate — it's fundamentally a consistency problem, not a skill problem. (More on the realistic workload in [microgreens maintenance](/microgreens-maintenance/).)

None of this means you shouldn't grow. It means honest DIY growing isn't truly "free." Your time, your counter space, and the occasional failed tray are part of the real cost — and they're the part the seed-price math never shows.
