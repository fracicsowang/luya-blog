If you love microgreens but wince at the checkout, you're not imagining it. A tiny 2 oz clamshell routinely runs **$4 to $6** — which works out to **$30–$50 per pound**, putting these baby greens among the most expensive produce in the store. And too often, they're already past their best by the time you get them home.

Here's why that happens, and what you can do about it.

## Why microgreens cost so much at retail

The high price isn't a markup conspiracy — microgreens are genuinely expensive to grow and sell at scale:

- **They're labor-intensive.** Seeding, monitoring, harvesting, and washing are largely done by hand, on short 7–14 day cycles.
- **They're fragile.** Tender greens bruise easily, so they need careful handling, refrigerated transport, and protective plastic packaging.
- **They have a brutal shelf life.** Most last only a few days after cutting, so retailers price in heavy spoilage and waste.
- **The supply chain is long.** Farm → packing → cold truck → distributor → store shelf → your fridge. Every step adds cost and time.

Put it on a chart and the gap is stark:

![Bar chart comparing the cost per pound of store-bought microgreens ($30–50) versus grown at home ($5–10)](/blog/img/charts/cost-store-vs-grow.svg)

That long supply chain is also why freshness suffers.

## The freshness problem nobody mentions

Microgreens are at their nutritional and flavor peak in the **first hours after harvest**. From the moment they're cut, they begin losing moisture, texture, and nutrients.

By the time a clamshell reaches you, it has often spent several days to a week in transit and on the shelf — sealed in plastic, slowly wilting. Here's the rough journey:

| Stage | Typical time | What's happening to the greens |
|---|---|---|
| Harvest & pack | Day 0 | Peak freshness and nutrient density |
| Cold transport & distribution | Days 1–3 | Moisture and nutrient loss begins |
| On the store shelf | Days 2–5 | Wilting accelerates in plastic |
| In your fridge | Days 1–4 | Often slimy before you finish the box |

You're frequently paying premium prices for greens already days past their prime. The sad, slightly slimy clamshell shoved to the back of the fridge is a familiar sight for anyone who buys microgreens regularly — and because they spoil so fast, much of what you buy never gets eaten, which makes the *effective* cost even higher.

## What you're really paying for

Add it up and a microgreens habit gets pricey fast. If you use microgreens a few times a week, you might go through 2–3 clamshells — call it **$10–18 a week**, or **$500–900 a year** — for greens that are wilting before you finish them.

For a food prized specifically for being fresh and nutrient-dense, that's a frustrating trade.
